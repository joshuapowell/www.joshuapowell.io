Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) Doesn't have to be Hard

In February, an announcement was posted to the Google Online Security Blog telling us that as of July 2018 all websites not using HTTPS would be marked as “Not Secure” .

For those who aren’t up on what this means, let’s stop for a minute and give a quick explanation. If your website or application has HTTPS in front of your URL, it means that your website has a Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) certificate installed on the server. This encrypts the traffic between the person requesting your site and the site itself, encrypting each transmission between the two. Most people buy these from commercial providers like Comodo, DigiCert, Symantec, or from their domain registrar and have the certificates installed by IT or their web administrator.

The sound of a “Not Secure” label on your web property sounds terrible. It sounds like Chrome is eroding your credibility. In reality, adding this type of simple certificate to your website increases the credibility and sets a higher standard for the internet as a whole.

We have always encouraged our partners to use SSL and in the past 18 months we’ve made it a prerequisite to launching any new application or API. What you quickly notice is that renewing and installing updated SSL certificates is not something that is top of mind. This always leads to one of those “Important, but Not-urgent” items that quickly becomes “Urgent and Important”. We wanted to make this process easier for our partners and so we started searching for a new way of buying and installing our SSL certificates.

We started by taking a look at the kinds of certificate authorities the bigger tech companies were using, Google, Github, Shopify, SquareSpace, and more. We found, to no surprise, Google is a Certificate Authority and therefore sign and issue their own SSL Certificates. Github and Shopify were using the same commercial CA that we were using, DigiCert. Finally, we had a look at SquareSpace and found that they were using certificates that were signed by AVG, the popular free Antivirus software. We dug deeper and discovered that Shopify was also using this same solution for the stores using their service.

Now we turned our focus to finding out where these AVG signed certificates were coming from. After some searching and digging around we found that these were certificates provided by Let’s Encrypt. We had heard about Let’s Encrypt when it launched in 2016 , but never gave it a serious look. We started testing these Let’s Encrypt certificates on our staging servers and comparing it against our existing certificate providers. What we found was that the Let’s Encrypt SSL Certificates were better and more secure that the certificates we were paying $200-300 per year for.

The past 8 months since our switch has flown by and we have switched all of our hosted cloud services to the use the Let’s Encrypt certificates. Their command line utilities and simple updates allow us to maintain control of the process and provide increased security to our partners.
